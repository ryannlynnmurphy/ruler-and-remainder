# A Cybersecurity Bill of Rights
### Rights as properties, not promises

## Preamble

Security is currently sold to you as a product — a subscription, a tier, a checkbox — and then sold back to you again, feature by feature, after it fails. This document refuses that arrangement. It treats security not as a service rendered but as a set of conditions owed: properties a system must have for you to be safe inside it.

One distinction organizes everything below. A *promise* is something a company says it will do. A *property* is something the system cannot avoid doing. A right that depends on a vendor's good behavior is not a right — it is a courtesy, revocable the moment it becomes inconvenient or unprofitable. Real rights live in architecture. They are decided at the level of how a thing is built, before anyone gets to choose whether to honor them.

So this is a bill of rights for users, written as a set of demands on the systems themselves.

---

## I. The right to know what the system does — and what it does not do.
Capabilities stated plainly; limits stated just as plainly. "Military-grade," "unbreakable," and "we take your security seriously" are not security controls. A system that overstates what it protects has already failed the first duty: telling you the truth about your own exposure.

## II. The right to data minimization by default.
A system may collect only what it needs to do the thing you asked it to do. Everything past that is surveillance wearing the costume of convenience. The burden is on the system to justify collection, never on you to opt out of it.

## III. The right to local processing.
Where a computation can run on a device you own, it should. Shipping your private life to someone else's datacenter is a choice a designer made — for their telemetry, their margins, their convenience — not a law of physics. Distance from your own data is not a feature.

## IV. The right to encryption that stays encrypted.
No backdoors, no "lawful access," no master keys held in escrow. A door built only for the good guys is still a door, and doors get found. Encryption you don't control, on hardware you don't own, is a promissory note, not a lock.

## V. The right to legible failure.
When a system is breached, it tells you — promptly, specifically, in language a person can act on. What was taken. What it means. What to do now. "An incident affecting certain users" is not a disclosure; it is a press release written by a lawyer to protect the company from you.

## VI. The right to refuse without penalty.
Protection is not a hostage. You do not pay a premium to *not* be tracked, and you are not downgraded for declining the data grab. Security cannot be the bait in an upsell.

## VII. The right to leave with everything.
Your data is portable and your history comes with you. Lock-in dressed as safety is not safety. A system you cannot exit has converted your security into its leverage.

## VIII. The right to inspection.
You, or someone you trust, can examine the systems you depend on. Security through obscurity protects the vendor's embarrassment, not your data. The ability to look is a precondition for the ability to trust.

## IX. The right to repair.
When something you own is vulnerable, you are allowed to fix it — or to pay someone other than the manufacturer to. A device that can only be secured by the company that shipped it insecure is not yours.

## X. The right to a human.
Any consequential security decision made about you can be contested to a person with the authority to reverse it. "The system flagged you" is the beginning of a conversation, not the end of one.

## XI. The right not to subsidize fragility.
Efficiency is a security property. A system that runs hot, over-collects, and over-provisions is not safer for its excess — it is more surface, more cost, more to defend, more to fail. You should not have to underwrite that bloat with your data, your battery, or the grid.

## XII. The right to be forgotten — actually.
Deletion means deletion: gone from backups, from logs, from the training set, from the partners it was quietly shared with. A delete button that only hides the data is a lie with a nice UI.

---

## Closing

None of these are gifts. Each describes a property a system can be built to have or built to lack — and the building is where the ethics already happened, long before any policy got written about it. A bill of rights like this is not finally asking *will you behave?* It is asking *what did you build?*
