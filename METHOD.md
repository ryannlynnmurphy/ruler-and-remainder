# Method

How this corpus is written, and how to keep one like it. The discipline is small
enough to state on one page and strict enough to change what you're willing to say.

## The three tiers

Every substantive claim is sorted into one of three tiers, and the tier is stated,
not implied.

- **Established.** Checkable now, against a source the reader can pull themselves. If it cannot survive someone going and looking, it is not established.
- **Exploratory.** Plausible and coherent, but untested at scale or not yet checked. Stated as a bet, with its conditions on the outside: *if* these things hold, *then* this follows.
- **Speculative.** Offered as geometry — a shape that organizes thinking. Useful for seeing, never load-bearing for a real system.

The one rule that makes the tiers worth anything: **established and speculative
never certify each other.** A speculative claim is not promoted because it sounds
profound. An established one is not stretched past what it actually shows because
the conclusion is attractive. The tiers are walls, not labels.

## Verbs carry the claim

Most overclaiming hides in a verb. A control that *detects* does not *prevent*. A
process that is *probabilistic* does not *guarantee*. A property of one component
is not a property of the whole system. Name the operation the thing actually
performs, and the claim corrects itself.

Common drifts to watch for:

- A probabilistic process described in the grammar of a guarantee.
- One component's property stretched across an entire system.
- A definite article doing silent work — "*the* layer" turning a control into a system.
- An unearned superlative — "first," "only," "unbreakable" — that the architecture has not paid for.

## Evidence and inference are kept apart

What was observed is written separately from what it is taken to mean. When a
source conflicts with another, that is marked, not resolved by tone. When something
is assumed, it is named as an assumption, not promoted to a fact because the rest of
the sentence needs it to be one.

## Say less, exactly

Legibility over volume. A claim made of honest, located parts is stronger than a
louder one. When in doubt, say less and say it precisely. The goal is not to sound
confident; it is to be readable — for the reader to be able to see exactly where
each claim stands and check it.

## Why this is the whole project

The corpus argues that power hides in the gap between what a system claims and what
it structurally does — and that the gap is invisible right up until it is tested in
public, when it comes due all at once. A method that refuses to let claims drift
past their evidence is the same argument, turned on the writing itself. The
discipline is not separate from the thesis. It *is* the thesis, practiced.
