01 every ruler leaves a remainder

start here: every ruler leaves a remainder.

when you measure something — a person, a price, a risk, a job — you reach for a tool. a category, a form, a score. and that tool was built by someone, for a purpose, to see certain things and not others.

02 the remainder lands on someone

the remainder is what the ruler can't read. and it doesn't vanish because the ruler ignores it — it lands on someone. usually the person the ruler wasn't built to see.

a credit score is built around a steady paycheck, a bank account, a long history. if your life runs on cash and gig work, the ruler doesn't read you as complex. it reads you as risky. you pay for that invisibility — a higher rate, or no loan at all.

so the whole body of work asks three questions: who built the ruler, what can't it read, and who pays for the remainder?

03 legibilism — make power readable

legibilism is the first move. every system hides something — it compresses the mess of reality into something you can navigate: a map, an interface, a price, a law. the compression is never neutral. someone chose what to show you and what to bury, and that choice never appears on a ballot.

one demand: make power readable — to the people living inside it.

04 attentionism — attention is the scarce thing

attentionism asks the harder question: readable to whom? a system everyone can see and nobody looks at is just an open secret.

attention — the capacity to notice what matters and act before the signal fades — is the scarce resource. not compute. not data. attention.

05 the three tiers — never let one pass for another

here's the spine. claims almost always sound more certain than the thing underneath can hold — in marketing, in institutions, in AI. the method measures that gap, and marks every single claim:

established you can check it now. exploratory plausible, untested. speculative a shape, not a proof.

and it never lets one pass for another. that discipline is the whole game.

06 vibe coding & machine arguing

last piece: how. all of this — the research, the software, this page you're reading — was built by arguing with a machine in plain language until something true fell out. ryann calls the arguing machine arguing.

you don't memorize syntax; you have a conversation and keep correcting it. it's called vibe coding and vibe research, and the point is the opposite of a flex: you can do this too.
