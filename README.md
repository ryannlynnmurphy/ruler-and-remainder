# Dorothy 

A research corpus and book by Ryann Murphy — the study of how systems read the
world into legible categories, what they cannot see, and who pays for what stays
invisible.

> Every system that survives learns how to read.

This is independent intellectual work: a way of thinking about machines, language,
and trust, developed on my own time and tools. It began in performance training —
the difference between what a character *says* and what their actions *reveal* —
and it ended up being about the same gap in software: the distance between what a
system claims and what it structurally does. Reading that gap is the whole project.

It is published openly so that anyone can read it, argue with it, and build on it.

Every essay carries its tier.
- **`website/`** — the launch document, the [Cybersecurity Bill of Rights](website/cybersecurity-bill-of-rights.md), and the essay *How I Got Into AI Security as a Former Theatre Major* ([`blog-theatre-major.md`](website/blog-theatre-major.md)).

## How to read it

The method is the point, not just the conclusions. Everything here is sorted into
three tiers, kept rigorously apart — see [`METHOD.md`](METHOD.md):

- **Established** — checkable now, against sources you can pull yourself.
- **Exploratory** — plausible, coherent, untested at scale. Offered as a bet, with its conditions worn on the outside.
- **Speculative** — offered as geometry: a shape that organizes thinking, never load-bearing for a real system.

Established and speculative never certify each other. A speculative claim cannot be
upgraded by how good it sounds, and an established one cannot be stretched past what
it actually shows. Reading the tier is reading the argument honestly.

## Using this as a template

If you want to keep a corpus this way — claims tiered, verbs accurate, the gap
between confidence and reality named rather than smoothed over — [`METHOD.md`](METHOD.md)
is written to be lifted out and reused. Fork it, replace the essays with your own,
and keep the discipline.
