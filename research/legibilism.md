# LEGIBILISM

### A Pamphlet

---

Every system hides something. That's what systems do. They take the mess of reality and compress it into a layer you can navigate. A map. An interface. A price. A law.

The compression is never neutral. Someone chose what to show you and what to bury. That choice is the most political act there is, and it never appears on a ballot.

Legibilism begins with one demand: **make power readable.**

---

## I. The Problem Is Not Left or Right

Left and right are seating arrangements from the French National Assembly, 1789. Supporters of the king sat to the right. Supporters of the revolution sat to the left. That's it. That's the entire framework the modern world uses to organize political thought.

It is a seating chart that went viral.

The actual political spectrum is not left to right. It is the distance between *what is* and *what ought to be* — and the question of who controls the system that manages the gap.

Every political project is an attempt to close that gap. Some say the gap is small and the system should preserve what exists. Some say the gap is vast and the system should force closure. But both are arguments about *systems* — who builds them, who they serve, how fast they move, who bears the cost of the transition.

This means politics has never been about values. It is about infrastructure. The values are just the story you tell about why your infrastructure should win.

---

## II. Abstraction Is the Political Act

A subscription service hides ownership. You never hold the thing. You access it, on their terms, forever.

A cloud platform hides compute. You never touch the server. You rent capacity through an API controlled by three companies.

A social media feed hides the algorithm. You never see the function that decides what you believe.

A zoning law hides capital. You never see why your rent doubled. You just see the new building.

These are all abstractions. They take complex systems and present a simplified surface. And in every case, the simplification serves someone. Not you. The entity that designed the layer.

This is not a conspiracy. It is architecture. Systems do what they are built to do. And the people who build them decide what gets shown and what gets hidden. That decision — the decision about legibility — is where power actually operates.

Not in elections. Not in debates. In the abstraction layer.

---

## III. Legibilism

Legibilism is the political commitment to making abstraction layers visible to the people living inside them.

It does not prescribe what ought to be. It insists that the system engineering the ought must be readable by the people it affects. You can disagree about the destination. You cannot hide the vehicle.

Three principles:

**1. If it hides ownership, tax it.**

A service that transfers a good to the buyer at the point of sale is commerce. A service that retains ownership and charges recurring access is extraction. The legal distinction should carry a tax distinction. If you won't let people own it, you pay more. This is not regulation. It is an incentive to stop building tollbooths.

**2. If it hides the route, open it.**

Protocols determine who has information, who has agency, and who is in the dark. Every closed protocol is a political decision disguised as engineering. Legibilism demands open protocols — not because openness is morally superior, but because closed systems make power illegible. You cannot hold accountable what you cannot see.

**3. If it hides the cost, show it.**

Every centralized system externalizes. The cloud externalizes heat. The platform externalizes labor. The subscription model externalizes risk onto the consumer. Legibilism demands that costs be visible at the point of decision. Not in a footnote. Not in a terms-of-service document nobody reads. In the interface. In the price. In the design.

---

## IV. The Middle Class Was a Legibility Project

Every stable democracy has a middle class underneath it. Not because middle-class people are morally superior. Because a middle class is what happens when surplus distributes widely enough that people have time to see clearly.

Time to think. Time to organize. Time to hold power accountable. Time to say no.

The middle class was never an income bracket. It was the material basis for *discernment* — the ability to look at a system and judge whether it is working.

That is being hollowed out. Not by accident. By architecture. The same concentration dynamics operating in compute infrastructure are operating in housing, healthcare, education, and labor. When you cannot afford to take a risk, you cannot start something. When you cannot afford to fail, you cannot dissent. The middle class disappears, and with it, the population's capacity to read power.

A legibilist rebuilds that capacity. Not by restoring a previous economy — the old basis for the middle class is being automated — but by engineering new systems that distribute agency. Local infrastructure. Open protocols. Cooperative ownership. The tools of legibility, made material.

---

## V. Intelligence Changes Nothing and Everything

Artificial intelligence can now perform the cognitive labor that was supposed to be the destination. Learn to code. Code writes itself. Learn to write. Writing writes itself. The ladder everyone was told to climb now automates from the top down.

This changes nothing about the political problem, which has always been: who owns the infrastructure and who pays the cost? AI does not answer that question. It makes it more urgent.

And it changes everything about the method, because intelligence is now cheap enough to model what ought to be. For the first time, you can rapidly prototype normative systems — test policies, simulate outcomes, iterate on design — at a speed that makes the gap between is and ought *navigable* rather than theoretical.

The question is who gets to navigate. If intelligence runs through three companies' APIs, the navigation concentrates. If it runs on local nodes with distributed ownership, it distributes. Same technology, opposite outcomes, determined by architecture.

Determined, that is, by the abstraction layer.

---

## VI. The Discipline

Legibilism is not only a politics. It is a research program.

The abstraction researcher studies how systems compress reality into navigable layers. They ask: what is this system hiding? Who benefits from the compression? What would change if the hidden layer were made visible?

This is not a new question. It is the oldest question in human culture, first asked by dramatists. The theater takes a world, compresses it into a set of constraints — characters, circumstances, conflict — and makes the compression *visible to an audience.* The audience watches the system operate in real time. They see what is hidden. They judge what ought to change.

The dramatist was the first abstraction researcher. Before philosophy, before the academy, before the Enlightenment organized knowledge into departments, humans gathered in public to watch systems perform and to vote on which performance told the truth.

Legibilism returns to that practice with new tools. Computation allows us to model systems at scale. Theater teaches us to make those models legible to a public. The combination — the artistic and the scientific, the *is* and the *ought*, held in a single discipline — is what the moment requires.

---

## VII. One Demand

Make power readable.

Make the subscription model say what it is. Make the protocol show who it serves. Make the platform reveal its cost. Make the algorithm face its audience.

This is not radical. It is the minimum condition for self-governance. You cannot have democracy if the systems that shape daily life are illegible to the people living inside them.

Every abstraction is an argument about what matters. Legibilism does not tell you which argument to make. It insists that the argument be visible.

The rest is politics. And politics, at last, can begin.
