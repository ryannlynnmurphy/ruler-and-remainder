# A Note on Method and Distribution

I came to this from theatre. I did not know what Python was, and I did not know how the research field worked — who you are supposed to know, what order you are supposed to do things in, which rooms you are supposed to wait outside of. So I skipped the waiting. I did the work, and I sent it to people.

That was not a strategy at first. It was just the obvious thing: if you make something and you think it is true, you show it to the people who would know whether it is true. I did not understand that this is not how it is usually done. By the time I understood, I had decided I preferred my way.

The method underneath all of this is the same in the work and in the sending of it. Do good work, in the open, and let the work carry itself. State what you can check and mark what you cannot. Do not claim more standing than you have — not because modesty is a virtue, but because the most useful reader is precisely the one calibrated to catch the gap between what you claim and what is actually there. Overclaiming your position is the same error this corpus spends its whole length describing in machines. I would rather be an outsider with checkable work than an insider with a borrowed reputation. The first is more interesting and harder to dismiss.

So: I am an independent researcher. No lab signs these pages. If a claim here is wrong, it is wrong in a way you can demonstrate, and I would like you to. That is the whole invitation.

I did not arrive at this through training. I arrived through feeling — strong, stubborn feeling about machines and who they are built to serve — and then taught myself enough of the infrastructure to act on it. *Radical Optimism* began with no idea how any of it worked and a refusal to let that stop me. The strength of feeling came first; the computer science came because the feeling demanded somewhere to go.

The thing I keep returning to is that the difference between love and hate is understanding. The same intensity runs both directions; what decides which one it becomes is whether you do the work of understanding the thing you feel so strongly about. Hate is feeling that refused to. Love is feeling that did. This corpus is that work — feeling taken all the way into understanding — and the wager underneath it is that the move transfers: that a conviction, understood all the way down, carries into AI governance, into sovereignty over the systems we live inside, into computational neuroscience. Whether it transfers is not mine to certify. It is in the work.

Check the math. Then decide.
