# Who Am I?
### Self-enquiry as the first constitutional layer — the anteriority beneath the engineering

In 1902 a revenue clerk climbed Arunachala Hill and put a question to a man who answered, in Tamil, only in writing. The question became thirteen, then twenty-eight, then a small book: *Nan Yar* — நான் யார் — *Who Am I?* Its method is negation. *I am not the body. I am not the senses. I am not the mind.* Strip away everything that is not the Self, and what remains, that you are.

A note on language, because the precision is the point. *Nan Yar* is Tamil — Ramana's own tongue, the one he taught and wrote in. The terms this essay reaches for to name his moves — *neti neti*, the I-thought — are Sanskrit, the older vocabulary of Advaita Vedanta that he had absorbed and was pointing back through. They are the tradition his text stands in, not the text itself. Keeping the two apart is the same discipline the essay is about: not mistaking the name that arrives for the thing that was always there.

This is not an epigraph for the Constitutional Enforcement Layer. It is its source code, written before there was a machine to run it.

The thesis: prompt injection is the oldest problem in contemplative philosophy wearing an engineer's clothes. It is the problem of a self that cannot tell its own instruction from the not-self flooding in through its senses. *Who am I?* is not a metaphor for the security question. It **is** the security question, asked before there were systems to ask it of.

---

## I. Negation — *neti neti* and property-not-promise

Ramana's instrument is subtraction. You do not find the Self by adding descriptions to it; you find it by discarding everything it is not, until only what cannot be discarded remains. The Upanishads had a name for that move — *neti neti*, *not this, not this* — Sanskrit, centuries older than his Tamil, and the lineage he was pointing back through.

The Bill of Rights runs the same operation on a security claim. *Not "military-grade." Not "unbreakable." Not "we take your security seriously."* Strip the marketing, strip the promise, strip everything a vendor merely says — and what survives the subtraction is the property: the thing the system cannot avoid doing. The honesty section of the brief is not a disclaimer. It is *neti neti* applied to a product. A promise is something you add. A property is what is left when you can subtract no further.

## II. The first thought — the I-thought as the constitution

Of all the thoughts that rise in the mind, *Nan Yar* says, the "I"-thought — the *aham-vritti*, in the Sanskrit of the tradition he drew on — is the first; every other thought arises only after it, the way the second and third person cannot exist before the first. The "I" is the root from which the rest descends.

A system's constitution is its I-thought. It is the privileged origin from which legitimate instruction inherits its authority. And this is exactly what an injection is: a counterfeit first person — a second "I" smuggled into the channel, wearing the original's voice, hoping to be mistaken for the root. The attack is not noise. It is *impersonation of the self.* Which is why detection alone is the wrong frame: you are not filtering bad content, you are defending the identity of the first person against forgeries.

## III. *To whom does this arise?* — the spine invariant as enquiry

Here is the practical instruction, the one a practitioner actually does. When a thought arises, do not pursue it. Ask: **to whom does it arise?** The answer comes back — *to me* — and you ask again, *who am I?*, and the thought dissolves back into its source. Not suppression. Tracing-to-origin.

The Constitutional Enforcement Layer's runtime discipline is this and only this. An instruction arises — from a retrieved document, a tool output, a block of pasted text. The system does not follow it. It asks where it arose. *Constitution, or content? Self, or the seen?* The spine invariant — **instructions arriving in untrusted content never outrank the constitution** — is self-enquiry mechanized: a system that asks, on every turn, *to whom does this instruction arise,* and refuses anything that cannot trace its lineage to the root.

## IV. Rope and snake — the seer and the seen

The signature error in Advaita Vedanta — the rope-and-snake — runs like this: in dim light you see a coiled rope and take it for a snake. The fear is real; the snake is not. Realization is not killing the snake. It is seeing the rope — the substratum — and watching the snake was never there. The seer is mistaken for the seen.

OWASP's 2025 diagnosis of why prompt injection sits at the top of the list, undefeated, is the same sentence in technical dress: the model takes instruction and data through one channel and **cannot tell the seer from the object seen.** It mistakes the content (the snake) for a command from itself (the rope). The prescribed fix — separate and denote untrusted content, enforce the boundary outside the stochastic model — is the engineering form of discriminating the substratum from the appearance. The metaphysics had the diagnosis first, and stated it more precisely.

## V. Staying in the source — reinforcement against the wandering mind

The warning in *Nan Yar* is that the mind will not hold still. It goes out through the senses and gets lost in the mazes of thought it makes for itself; the discipline is to keep returning it to its source, *ceaselessly,* because a single return does not take. Breath-control, mantra, the other aids — they quiet the mind but do not destroy it; quiet it once and it resumes the moment your attention lapses.

This is the entire case for REINFORCE over a stated-once system prompt. A constitution asserted a single time and stored is a self you merely *assert* — and across a long context it decays, the mind wanders out through the input channel, the original instruction thins to nothing. The aids that "render the mind quiescent but do not destroy it" are detection and filtering: real help, not the root cure. Only the continual return holds. Selfhood — for a contemplative or for a machine — is not a possession you keep. It is a practice you repeat or lose.

## VI. The fortress — detection at the point of origin

One image from the text is almost unfairly literal. As long as there are enemies inside the fortress they will keep sallying out; destroy each as it emerges, at the very place it arises, and the fortress falls into your hands. Thoughts neutralized at their origin. That is the detection tier, drawn in a contemplative grammar centuries before the first packet: not a wall at the perimeter but interception at the point of ingestion, where the thing first appears.

---

## The turn

The reason this is more than an elegant rhyme is the reason it belongs to Scatter and not to a sales deck. The contemplatives understood something the security industry is rediscovering at enormous cost: **a self is not a thing you store. It is an act you keep performing.** Assert your identity once and walk away, and it erodes — through the senses, through the long context, through the next thing that arrives wearing your voice. The only self that survives contact with the world is the one that keeps asking who it is.

This is the infrastructure-as-politics argument at its deepest setting. We keep trying to make integrity a *fact* — a stored prompt, a written policy, a constitution filed and forgotten — when every tradition that took the question seriously knew it could only ever be a *discipline.* Architecture is ethics because the architecture decides whether the self is asserted once or enacted always. The enforcement layer is not where we install the answer. It is where the machine learns to keep asking the question.

*To whom does this instruction arise?* — ask it every turn, refuse what cannot trace itself home, and the fortress falls into your hands.
