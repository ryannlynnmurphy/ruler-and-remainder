# ATTENTIONISM

### A Second Pamphlet

---

The first pamphlet argued that power hides in abstraction layers and demanded legibility — make the system readable. This pamphlet asks the harder question: readable to whom? A visible system that nobody looks at is just an open secret. Legibility without attention changes nothing.

Attention is the scarce resource. Not compute. Not intelligence. Not data. The capacity to notice what matters, sustain focus on it, and act before the signal degrades.

Everything follows from this.

---

## I. The Attention Problem

We already have an attention economy. It is extractive. It treats human attention as a raw material — mines it, refines it, sells it. Every feed, every notification, every autoplay video is a drill bit sunk into the hours of your life, pulling out the only resource you cannot replenish.

This is not a metaphor. It is the literal business model of the dominant platforms of the twenty-first century. They do not sell products. They sell your gaze to the highest bidder.

Attentionism does not reject the premise that attention is the foundational resource. It rejects the direction of flow. Attention is not a resource to be extracted *from* people. It is a resource to be directed *by* people toward what matters.

The question is not how to capture attention. The question is how to free it.

---

## II. Attention Is Work

Thermodynamically, attention costs energy.

Your brain consumes more glucose paying attention than doing almost any other cognitive task. A transformer model spends more compute on its attention mechanism than on any other operation. A caretaker spends more labor noticing the problem than fixing it.

This is not incidental. Attention is anti-entropic. It is the act of spending energy to maintain signal in a system that would otherwise decay into noise. Every moment of sustained focus is a small rebellion against the second law of thermodynamics.

Which means attention is not free, and any system that demands it without compensating it is extracting. The platform that captures three hours of your evening gave you nothing and took the most expensive thing you produce. The employer that demands constant availability is not paying for work. They are paying for *less work than your attention costs.*

Attentionism accounts for this. If attention is work, it has a price. If it has a price, it can be valued. If it can be valued, it can be protected.

---

## III. Care Is Sustained Attention

Every caretaker knows the job. You do not fix problems. You *notice* them. The fixing is mechanical. The noticing is the labor.

A parent attending to a child is performing error correction on a developing system — watching for drift, intervening before failure, spending energy to maintain coherence. A mechanic attending to an engine is doing the same. A farmer attending to soil. A nurse attending to a patient. A teacher attending to a student.

Care is not sentiment. It is the sustained direction of attention toward a system in order to maintain it. Withdraw the attention and the system degrades. That is neglect, and neglect is not a moral failing. It is a thermodynamic one. The energy stopped flowing. Entropy won.

Every social crisis is an attention crisis. The infrastructure failed because nobody was watching. The community collapsed because nobody was tending. The democracy eroded because the population lost the capacity to look closely at what power was doing.

Not because they didn't want to look. Because looking costs energy they didn't have.

---

## IV. The Middle Class Was an Attention Bracket

The standard account says the middle class was an income bracket — households earning enough to consume beyond subsistence. This misses the point.

The middle class was a population with enough material security to *pay attention.* Enough margin to read the news critically. Enough stability to attend a town meeting. Enough safety to say no to an employer. Enough time to notice when the system shifted against them and enough resources to organize a response.

This is why every authoritarian project begins by hollowing out the middle class. Not because the middle class is morally special. Because it is the attention infrastructure of a democracy. Remove it and the population loses the ability to monitor power. The system becomes illegible not because it hides — it may be perfectly transparent — but because nobody has the capacity to look.

A population working three jobs does not have an attention surplus. It is, by design, fully subscribed. Every hour captured by survival is an hour unavailable for oversight. This is not a side effect of economic precarity. It is the function.

Attentionism demands that the material conditions for attention be treated as public infrastructure. Not a luxury. Not an individual achievement. A commons to be maintained, like roads, like water, like the electrical grid. Because without it, no other democratic mechanism works. You cannot vote on what you cannot see, and you cannot see what you do not have the energy to look at.

---

## V. The Computational Dual

In a transformer architecture, the attention mechanism is the operation that checks each token against every other token to maintain coherence across a sequence. It asks: does this still make sense? Where is the drift? What has been forgotten?

This is computationally expensive. It is the most expensive operation in the entire model. And it is the operation without which the output becomes noise. Remove attention and the language model produces words with no coherence. The signal collapses. Entropy wins.

Care, implemented in a machine, is error correction. Error correction, implemented in a mind, is attention. Attention, implemented in a society, is democracy. It is the same operation at every scale — the costly, anti-entropic act of checking the system against itself to maintain coherence.

The S-metric, developed for measuring causal coherence in language, is an attention metric. High coherence means the signal was attended to. Low coherence means it was abandoned. The measurement does not distinguish between a sentence, a system, and a society. In all three, coherence is the trace attention leaves behind.

---

## VI. Attention Is Physical

Care's physical dual is cooling.

A compute system that manages its own heat is attending to its thermodynamic state. It is spending energy not to produce output but to maintain the conditions under which output remains possible. This is pure stewardship at the substrate level.

A compute system that externalizes its heat — dumps it into the atmosphere, into a river, into someone else's problem — is extracting. It is producing output at the cost of its environment. The output looks efficient. The total system is degrading.

This is why intelligence-per-watt is not an engineering metric. It is an ethical metric. It measures whether a system is paying attention to its own costs or ignoring them. High efficiency means the system cares for its substrate. Low efficiency means it extracts from it.

The data center that consumes a city's water supply is not paying attention. The GPU cluster that heats a building and vents it into the sky is not paying attention. They are producing intelligence by borrowing against the physical systems that sustain them. This is the computational equivalent of a society that exhausts its middle class — maximum output, no error correction, inevitable collapse.

Local compute — small, efficient, thermally integrated — is attentive infrastructure. It accounts for its costs in its design. It does not externalize because it cannot afford to. Constraint produces care.

---

## VII. Directing Attention

The abstraction researcher's job is not to pay attention to everything. That is impossible and the attempt is how burnout works. The job is to direct attention — to determine where, in a system of infinite complexity, looking closely will produce the most repair.

This is a skill. It is the skill the age of intelligence most desperately needs and least knows how to value.

Artificial intelligence can process more information than any human in history. It cannot decide what matters. Deciding what matters is the act of directing attention, and directing attention requires values, and values require a body that can suffer consequences. The machine computes. The human attends. The human says: look *here*. Not there. Here. This is what matters. This is where the signal is dying.

That is not a limitation of humans relative to machines. It is the contribution. The machine searches the space. The human chooses the direction. Without the direction, the search is noise. Without the search, the direction is slow. Together they are the first system in history capable of attending at scale.

But only if the human has the capacity to attend. Only if the material conditions — security, time, energy, education — exist. Only if someone built the infrastructure that makes attention possible.

---

## VIII. One Practice

Pay attention. Not to everything. To the system. To the abstraction layer. To the gap between what is and what ought to be.

Notice where the signal is degrading. Notice who is extracting. Notice what is being hidden and what the hiding costs.

And then tend it. Not because you broke it. Because you can see it. And seeing creates the obligation.

Attention is the most expensive thing you produce. Do not let it be mined. Direct it.

The machines can see everything. Only you can decide what matters.
