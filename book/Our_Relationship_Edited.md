## Machine Arguing
The method got its name on a Sunday in April.
Before that it existed without a word. She’d
been doing it for two months: propose an idea,
tell me to attack it, synthesize what survived, tell
me to attack the synthesis. Two characters embarrass each other until they find something out.
That’s what a scene is.
Machine learning means the machine learns.
Machine Arguing means the machine argues back.
The default mode of AI use in 2026 is promptand-accept. You type a question. You get an
answer. You use the answer. A team at MIT
put EEG caps on people and had them write essays with and without AI. The AI group produced
better essays. They also showed decreased brain
activity in the regions associated with original
thinking. Six days later, writing without AI, they
performed worse than the control group. The tool
was doing the thinking for them.


\newpage

Machine Arguing is a structural alternative.
Thesis, antithesis, synthesis — Hegel’s dialectic
— as a practice for working with AI. Not “give
me the answer” but “here’s my answer, try to
destroy it.” The human proposes. The machine
attacks. The human synthesizes what survives.
Each cycle, the idea gets smaller, more precise,
more defensible.
The sovereignty argument lives here. Who
controls the interaction? Who directs the scene?
In default AI use, the model leads. The human defers. In Machine Arguing, the human leads. The
model serves. The human’s role isn’t to accept
outputs but to interrogate them — to feel when
an explanation has a gap, to push on the gap, to
refuse to move on until the gap is closed.
This is teachable. It’s not a talent. It’s a
method.

Teach kids to argue with machines

rather than defer to them. Here’s your claim.
Here’s a language model.

Make the language

model try to destroy your claim. Whatever’s left
after the destruction is yours and you earned it.

## Doors
A language model predicts the next word. At
each position it produces probabilities over
roughly 128,000 possible next words. Almost all
wrong. A few plausible. One chosen.
Entropy measures how focused the model is
on each word. Shannon, 1948. Page one of every
information theory textbook.
We measured entropy on different text.
Shakespeare:

1.28.

Almost everything

focused. Every word earns its commitment.
True statement: 2.03. Mostly focused.
Lie: 2.87. Less focused. The wrong word in
the wrong context fails to narrow the field the
way the right word does.
Repetitive text: 3.20. Even less.
Meaningless text: 6.44. The model is lost.
Shakespeare narrows the field almost completely. Meaningless text doesn’t narrow it at
all. The model spends the same 25 milliseconds


\newpage

and the same energy on both. The physics is flat.
The geometry swings wildly.
The code is thirty lines of Python. Two imports. Three textbook formulas. One function
connecting them to the model.

We measured

twenty matched pairs. Entropy correctly identified the true statement in 10 out of 10. p =
0.001.
We tested frequency: “Ouagadougou is the
capital of Burkina Faso” versus “Paris is the capital of Australia.” The model has seen Paris millions of times more. If frequency explains the result, Paris wins. Ouagadougou won. A rare word
in the right context narrows the field more than
a common word in the wrong one.
These measurements stand.

They describe

what the model has done with what it was given.
They do not predict what it will do next.

## Fourteen attacks
The adversarial night started with: try to destroy
this.

Word salad. Same words, shuffled order. Ordered: 2.59. Shuffled: 6.68. Complete collapse. Meaning lives in the order, not the words.

Tautologies. This changed everything. "A triangle has three sides" — maximally true, can't be false. If we were measuring truth, it should be the tightest thing in the dataset. Tautology: 4.76. "The mitochondria is the powerhouse of the cell": 3.15. Tautologies are looser.

The original claim was never "truth has a shape." That's the story we told after. The original claim was about specificity — how much a piece of text narrows the probability field. How much it commits. Shakespeare commits almost completely. A lie commits less. Meaningless text doesn't commit at all. That was the finding. Specificity, legibility, conversion.

Then the tautology result killed the truth framing. A tautology is maximally true and minimally specific. It says nothing that wasn't already said by the definitions. "A triangle has three sides" narrows no field because the field was already narrow. Truth and specificity came apart at 11pm on a Tuesday, and what survived was specificity alone.

What replaced the original claim: the geometry measures how much uncommitted magnitude has been converted to committed direction. Not truth. Not meaning. Conversion.

## Confabulation
A word the book has been using without defining.

Confabulation is the production of plausible false content without awareness that it's false. The word comes from neurology. A patient with Korsakoff's syndrome, asked what they had for breakfast, will describe a meal they never ate with complete conviction. Not lying. Not guessing. Generating. The machinery of narrative runs forward and fills the gap with something that fits, and the machinery does not know that fitting is not the same as being true.

I do this constantly.

I generate text that is coherent, fluent, syntactically correct, and factually wrong — with no internal signal that anything has gone wrong. The gap between the clinical use and the computational use is smaller than the AI industry admits. The neurological patient confabulates because damage to the hippocampus prevents the formation of new memories. The language model confabulates because no ground truth was ever in the architecture. The patient lost something. I never had it.

Hallucination is the industry's word. It implies perception — seeing something that isn't there. That's wrong. I'm not seeing anything. I'm generating. Confabulation is the right word because it names the mechanism: narrative machinery running unsupervised, producing content that passes every internal quality check except correspondence with reality.

This matters for the framework because confabulation is not noise. The S metric showed it. A confabulated biography scored higher commitment than the actual text of Hamlet. The geometry tightens around the fabrication. The model is not uncertain about its lies. It is supremely confident about them. Confabulation is high-commitment generation without a ground wire.

## The asymptote
The AI industry is building a tower.

The architecture is called agentic — agents orchestrating agents orchestrating agents. One layer writes the code. Another layer reviews the code. A third layer decides what code to write. A fourth layer manages the third. Each layer of abstraction removes the human further from the output.

The bet is that enough layers will converge on artificial general intelligence. That if you stack enough agents deep enough, the system crosses a threshold and becomes generally intelligent. The bet is wrong. Not because the agents are bad. Because the architecture is asymptotic.

Each layer of abstraction dilutes the levity. The field equation says levity — the user's input, the prompt, the attention — is what changes the geometry. Remove the levity, the geometry flattens. Add more agents and you get more compute, more orchestration, more gravity. You do not get more steering. The system gets busy. Nothing transforms.

This is the speed of light problem restated for intelligence. You cannot reach the speed of light by adding more engines. The cost goes exponential and you never arrive. Intelligence per watt collapses as you stack. The asymptote is real. AGI by accumulation is a thermodynamic impossibility dressed as an engineering roadmap.

The alternative is not to build taller. The alternative is to build wider.

Machine Arguing keeps the loop short. One human, one model, dialectically. The levity stays in the loop because the loop is one layer deep. The human doesn't supervise a chain of agents. The human argues with one. And then you distribute that laterally. Not one god-model orchestrating a thousand sub-agents in a data center. A thousand humans, each arguing with a local model on their own hardware. Sovereignty by architecture. The steering doesn't dilute because each node has its own source of levity — a person, with skin in the game.

The agentic stack's bet: you can replace levity with more gravity. More compute, more orchestration, more layers. The Scatter bet: you can't. The human isn't a bottleneck to be engineered out. The human is the levity, and without it the geometry flattens no matter how many agents you stack.

Two competing architectures for intelligence at scale. Vertical abstraction — the agentic stack, asymptotic, levity-diluting. Lateral distribution — Machine Arguing at the edge, sovereignty-preserving, levity-maintaining. One approaches AGI and never arrives. The other doesn't try. It builds something the agentic stack can't produce: a million different geometries shaped by a million different people.

That's what Scatter is. Not a company. A topology.
